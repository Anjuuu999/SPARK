Nature & Green Spaces

Forests, parks, and urban greenery aren't just nice to look at they're one of our most powerful tools against climate change. This page looks at why green spaces matter, what's at risk, and what you can actually do about it.

  • Forests
  • Urban Green Spaces
  • Tree Planting
  • Biodiversity

Forests

Forests absorb roughly a third of the carbon dioxide released by burning fossil fuels each year, acting as a natural buffer against climate change. When forests are cut down or burned, that stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere.

Forests also support the biodiversity that keeps ecosystems stable enough to handle a changing climate.

Support reforestation projects and choose FSC-certified wood/paper products.

Urban Green Spaces

Cities are often several degrees hotter than surrounding rural areas, the urban heat island effect, caused by concrete and a lack of vegetation. Parks and street trees cool cities by providing shade and releasing moisture into the air.

Green spaces also improve air quality and support wellbeing for people living in dense urban areas.

Tree Planting

Community tree-planting drives, when done at scale with the right species for the local environment, genuinely help restore green cover, support biodiversity, and cool neighbourhoods over time.

As a student, you don't need land or money to get involved, just a few hours at a local event.

Check the Feedback & Program Directory page for local tree-planting drives.

Biodiversity

Diverse ecosystems are more resilient, a mix of plant and animal species helps a green space recover faster from floods, droughts, and extreme heat than a single-species area can.

Protecting biodiversity in green spaces near you helps the wider climate system stay balanced.

Choose native plants if you garden, they support local wildlife far better than imported species.
